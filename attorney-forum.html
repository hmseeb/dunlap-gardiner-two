HOA Law

Fining Homeowners: Does Your Board Have the Authority?

One of the most common questions we receive from HOA boards is whether they have the power to fine homeowners for covenant violations. The answer depends entirely on your governing documents.

Many associations assume they can assess fines simply because they have rules in place. However, for fines to be enforceable in Georgia, your covenants must contain an "enabling clause" that explicitly grants the board fining authority. Without this language, monetary penalties against homeowners may be unenforceable in court — even if the violation is clear-cut.

Before implementing or enforcing fines, every HOA board should:

  • Review governing documents for an express fining authority clause
  • Ensure fine schedules are adopted through proper procedure
  • Provide proper notice and opportunity to cure before assessing fines
  • Maintain consistent enforcement across all homeowners

If your covenants are silent on fining authority, it may be time to consider an amendment. Our attorneys can help review your documents and guide your board through the amendment process.

Legislative Update

SB 46: Georgia's HOA Common Area Legislation

In 2017, Partner Brian Gardiner drafted legislation that was subsequently signed into law by Governor Nathan Deal. SB 46 addressed two critical issues facing Georgia homeowner associations:

  • Abandoned Common Area Control: Providing a legal mechanism for associations to gain control over common areas that have been abandoned by developers
  • Election Establishment Procedures: Creating clearer procedures for establishing and managing HOA election processes

This legislative achievement reflects Dunlap Gardiner's deep commitment to community association law — not only representing clients in individual matters, but actively shaping the legal landscape that governs Georgia communities.

If your association has questions about how SB 46 or other recent legislation may affect your operations, contact Brian Gardiner directly.
